Open Data Policy and Portal

DataCuse

In May 2017 the City of Syracuse released a draft Open Data Policy I authored with input from department heads and commissioners and help from the Sunlight Foundation and Center for Government Excellence and Johns Hopkins University as part of the City's agreement with the Bloomberg Philanthropies funded What Works Cities program. The policy was released for public comment on the Madison platform created by the Open Government Foundation. The draft policy and comments are located here.

Following the public comment period, we finalized the policy - available here.

In July 2017, the City of Syracuse launched its first Open Data Portal, called DataCuse. The portal includes data about a variety of topics, including housing, infrastructure, and city boundaries. Additionally, the website has pages dedicated to making the data more understandable for users who might not want to only see raw data, and would rather see maps and charts.

Housing Data Warehouse

The City of Syracuse has data about its housing, building, and parcels that live in a variety of data sources. Various efforts have been made to centralize this data, much of which has been time consuming.

In the summer of 2017, along with an intern, we build data pipelines using SQL, Python, and Microsoft SSIS to automate the extraction, transformation, and loading of data into a central data warehouse. City departments can now use this data, it is used to power the Open Data Portal, as well as other data applications.

Building the data warehouse likely saves time for many, but specifically it saves at least eight to 16 hours per month for one department that used to rely on an employee to create a dataset that incorporates data from different sources.

This data warehouse includes data from the Codes department, assessment, police, water billing, tax and finance, census, and zoning.

Automated pothole collection

The City of Syracuse fills a lot of potholes. Many of the potholes filled are based on complaints, but the crews fill others that they see while out in the field. There had not been a way to collect digital data about where they were filling potholes, which meant they could not report how much work they were doing.

The City places GPS units on all of its Department of Public Works vehicles. These GPS units show where the truck is, and also log when it performs and action. When the pothole filling trucks fill a pothole, the crew member triggers a hose that shoots emulsion. When that trigger is pulled, the GPS unit logs a row of data.

As a result of a script I wrote, each night, data from the GPS unit is filtered and transformed so we can see where and when every pothole was filled. It result in a map like this.

We now know that the city is filling thousands of potholes per year, and we can say exactly where they were filled. This data can also be used to assess road quality and help plan for future repairs.
